La Traviata

Giuseppe Verdi's "La Traviata" is one of the most beloved and performed operas in the world. Based on Alexandre Dumas' play "La Dame aux Camélias," it tells the tragic story of the courtesan Violetta Valéry and her doomed love affair with Alfredo Germont.

Composer: Giuseppe Verdi
Premiered: March 6, 1853 at La Fenice in Venice, Italy
Language: Italian
Setting: Paris and its environs, around 1850
Duration: Approximately 2 hours and 30 minutes
View Performances Read Full Synopsis

Synopsis: La Traviata

Act I

Violetta Valéry, a famed courtesan, throws a lavish party at her Paris salon to celebrate her recovery from an illness. Gastone, a viscount, introduces her to Alfredo Germont, a young bourgeois from a provincial family who has long admired Violetta from afar. Alfredo raises a toast to Violetta, and she is touched by his genuine interest. As the guests move to the ballroom to dance, Violetta suffers a fainting spell. Alfredo stays behind and declares his love. At first, Violetta rejects him, saying that love means nothing to her. However, something about Alfredo's sincerity touches her, and she gives him a camellia, telling him to return when the flower has wilted. After the guests leave, Violetta wonders if Alfredo could be the man she could truly love.

Act II

Three months later, Alfredo and Violetta are living together in a country house outside Paris. Violetta has abandoned her former life. Alfredo learns from the maid, Annina, that Violetta has been selling her possessions to support their lifestyle. Ashamed, he leaves for Paris to settle matters. While he is gone, Giorgio Germont, Alfredo's father, arrives and asks Violetta to end her relationship with his son. He explains that Violetta's reputation has threatened his daughter's engagement. Violetta is heartbroken but agrees, asking only that Alfredo's sister be told of her sacrifice after her death. She writes a farewell letter to Alfredo. When Alfredo returns, Violetta tells him she no longer loves him and is returning to her old life. Alfredo is devastated and his father attempts to comfort him, but Alfredo finds an invitation from Violetta's friend Flora and decides to confront Violetta at the party.

Act III

At Flora's party, Alfredo publicly humiliates Violetta by throwing his gambling winnings at her feet as payment for her services. Giorgio Germont, who has followed his son, witnesses this and denounces Alfredo's behavior. Baron Douphol, Violetta's escort, challenges Alfredo to a duel. Six months later, Violetta is dying of consumption in her Paris apartment, attended only by Annina and Dr. Grenvil. She reads a letter from Giorgio Germont telling her that Alfredo knows of her sacrifice and is coming to beg her forgiveness. Alfredo arrives and the lovers are reunited, planning to leave Paris together. However, it is too late for Violetta, who dies in Alfredo's arms, declaring that her pain and suffering have ended.

Historical Context

"La Traviata" premiered at La Fenice opera house in Venice on March 6, 1853. The opera was initially considered a failure, partly due to the casting of a soprano who was considered too old and overweight to play the consumptive Violetta. However, when it was revived a year later with a different cast, it became a great success.

The opera was revolutionary for its time as it dealt with contemporary social issues rather than historical or mythological subjects. It portrayed the life of a courtesan, a subject considered scandalous for the opera stage in the mid-19th century. Verdi insisted on setting the opera in contemporary times, though the censors initially forced him to set it in the past to make it less controversial.

"La Traviata" is based on the novel and play "La Dame aux Camélias" by Alexandre Dumas fils, which was in turn inspired by the real-life courtesan Marie Duplessis, with whom Dumas had a brief affair before her death from tuberculosis at the age of 23. The character of Violetta Valéry is based on Duplessis.

The opera reflects the social attitudes of the time towards "fallen women" and the double standards applied to men and women in matters of morality. It also portrays the conflict between genuine love and the societal expectations of class and propriety.

Musically, "La Traviata" represents Verdi's middle period and showcases his evolving style. It features some of his most beautiful and memorable melodies, including Violetta's aria "Sempre libera" and the duet "Un dì felice." The opera's emotional depth and psychological complexity mark it as one of Verdi's masterpieces.

Today, "La Traviata" remains one of the most performed operas worldwide and continues to move audiences with its beautiful music and tragic love story.
